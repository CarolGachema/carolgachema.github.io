Psychedelic Neuroplasticity and the Neurotrophic Architecture of Pancreatic Cancer (Convergent Pathways)

Translational Oncology
Bioinformatics

2026 · TCGA-PAAD

Overview

Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) is highly aggressive and increasingly recognized to interact with the nervous system. Neurotrophin receptors such as NTRK1 and NTRK2 may influence tumour progression, yet their expression patterns in PDAC remain incompletely characterised.

TCGA-PAAD transcriptomic and survival data were analysed to investigate neurotrophin receptor expression, co-expression patterns, and clinical relevance.

Key Findings:

  • Confirmed expression of both NTRK1 and NTRK2 across PDAC tumours
  • Demonstrated significant co-expression (Spearman ρ = 0.416, p < 0.001)
  • Showed MAPK and PI3K-Akt signalling pathways are significantly enriched in the NTRK2 co-expression network
  • Provided survival analysis by expression group

Alzheimer’s Disease Machine Learning Classifier

Machine Learning Neurodegeneration

2026 · ADNI Dataset

Overview

Alzheimer’s disease is the most common neurodegenerative disorder worldwide, yet its molecular mechanisms remain incompletely understood. This project aimed to identify key genes associated with Alzheimer’s disease and investigate whether gene expression signatures could be used to accurately classify disease status.

To evaluate the predictive potential of these genes, I developed multiple machine learning models in Python, including Random Forest, Support Vector Machine, Logistic Regression, and Gradient Boosting classifiers. Random Forest emerged as the best-performing model, achieving a cross-validation accuracy of 90.7%, test accuracy of 87.9%, and ROC-AUC of 0.9038.

Intersystem Noise Coupling

Signal Analysis Instrumentation

2026

Overview

This project proposes a novel computational framework called Intersystem Noise Coupling, which investigates whether biological “noise” the small fluctuations and variability present within physiological signals, may act as a mechanism of coordination between organ systems.

Rather than treating physiological noise as unwanted randomness, the framework explores the possibility that stochastic fluctuations carry meaningful information capable of synchronizing activity across multiple organs.

The project aims to utilise large-scale physiological datasets, including MMASH and MIMIC-IV, to analyse time-series signals from multiple organ systems simultaneously.

Approaches

  • Stochastic modelling
  • Cross-correlation analysis
  • Time-series decomposition
  • Network dynamics
  • Physiological synchronization metrics
  • Noise propagation modelling

MMASH Dataset

Heart rate and locomotion signals were modelled as coupled stochastic oscillators using an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck (OU) process framework.

The idealized healthy simulation (κ_xy = 0.5) produces correlated heart-locomotion time series and a structured phase space attractor — the signature of two systems influencing each other.

The MMASH dataset, by contrast, yields a coupling coefficient of κ_xy = −0.0028: effectively zero. The phase space trajectory is an unstructured random walk, indicating that cardiac and locomotion signals in healthy wearable recordings behave as statistically independent processes at the timescales captured.

The windowed analysis across subjects confirms this picture. For the majority of each recording, κ values for both directions hover near zero. Coupling is not absent by definition, brief transient episodes of stronger coupling appear episodically, and the dominant direction (locomotion-to-cardiac vs cardiac-to-locomotion) varies across subjects and time windows. Some subjects show sustained negative κ_xy during specific periods, suggesting inhibitory dynamics rather than simple uncoupling.

Key tools: Python · scikit-learn · pandas · numpy · matplotlib · ADNI
