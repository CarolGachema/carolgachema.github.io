CCR5Δ32 Mutation Gap: The HIV Stem Cell Cure Is Nearly Irrelevant for Sub-Saharan Africa

Publishing in progress WHO-Directed 2025–2026

Overview

The paper is a structural and bio-ethical critique of the current global HIV cure paradigm. It argues that while the scientific community celebrates allogeneic hematopoietic stem cell transplantation (allo-HSCT) as a landmark proof of concept, this “cure” is a biological and logistical illusion for Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA), the region that bears over two-thirds of the global HIV burden. The core thesis is that the cure is built on an accidental, Euro centric genetic framework that completely excludes, underfunds, and ignores the populations that need a HIV cure the most

The Problem

  1. Complete Absence of the Allele - The cure relies entirely on a donor possessing the homozygous \(CCR5\Delta32\) genetic mutation, which stops HIV from entering host cells.
  • While this mutation exists in about 1% of Northern Europeans, large-scale genomic data shows its frequency is precisely 0% in studied populations across Angola, Nigeria, Zimbabwe, and Ethiopia.The HLA Matching Compound Problem:
  • Because successful transplants require strict Human Leukocyte Antigen (HLA) tissue matching to prevent fatal graft-versus-host disease, an African patient cannot simply use a European donor.

  1. The Cost Wall - A single allo-HSCT procedure costs between $100,000 and $300,000. The procedure demands specialized sterile bone marrow units, advanced blood banking, high-resolution HLA typing tech, and intensive care units equipped for extreme toxicities. These resources are fundamentally absent or severely constrained in most Sub-Saharan healthcare systems.

  2. The Cancer Requirement - This procedure has never been a standalone treatment for HIV; it is an incredibly aggressive, last-resort intervention for patients who also have life-threatening blood cancers (like leukemia).

Key Recommendations

The paper makes the following recommendations directed at the WHO and trial sponsors:

  1. Commission an SSA - Focused Cure Agenda: Shift funding to study CCR5-independent remission mechanisms and invest in gene therapies with low-income access guidelines built into the initial trial phases.

  2. Diversify Global Donor Registries - Launch targeted, well-funded recruitment drives for African, Asian, and Latin American donors to fix the massive European bias in global HLA matching data.

  3. Establish Regional Centers of Excellence - Fund the construction of at least five regional advanced hematological and gene therapy medical hubs across Sub-Saharan Africa to serve both cure research and broader oncological needs.

  4. Enforce Advance Market Commitments (AMCs) - Mandate tiered licensing agreements guaranteeing that when a viable, population-agnostic cure is found, it is affordable and accessible to LMICs immediately upon regulatory approval, preventing the multi-decade delay that occurred with early antiretroviral drugs.

Why This Matters

Because all human beings deserve treatment that works for them.

Neuropathological Consequences of Emerging Viral Pathogens in East Africa

Institute for Global Excellence Conference 2026

Overview

This review examined current evidence regarding the neuropathological effects of emerging viral diseases affecting East African populations, with particular attention to mechanisms of central nervous system injury, neuroinflammation, and long-term neurological sequelae.

The review synthesised findings from studies involving viral pathogens associated with neurological complications, including emerging infectious diseases capable of crossing the blood-brain barrier and inducing direct or indirect damage to neural tissues.

Key Themes - Neuroinflammation as a common mechanism of neurological injury. - Blood-brain barrier disruption during viral infection. - Direct viral invasion of neural tissue. - Long-term neurological and cognitive sequelae following infection. - Challenges in surveillance, diagnosis, and management within resource-limited settings.

Significance

  • The review highlights the need to expand infectious disease research beyond acute clinical outcomes and consider the long-term neurological burden associated with emerging pathogens.

  • By focusing on East Africa, the paper emphasizes regional research gaps and underscores the importance of strengthening neurological surveillance, research capacity, and healthcare preparedness for future outbreaks.
